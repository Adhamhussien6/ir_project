Ancient Egypt: Civilization of the Nile

Ancient Egypt was one of the world's earliest and most enduring civilizations, flourishing along the Nile River in northeastern Africa for over 3,000 years. Its history spans from around 3100 BCE, when Upper and Lower Egypt were unified under the first pharaoh, to 30 BCE when it was conquered by Rome.

The Nile River was the lifeblood of ancient Egyptian civilization. Its annual flooding deposited rich silt on the surrounding lands, enabling agriculture in an otherwise desert region. The Egyptians divided their world into the "Black Land" of fertile Nile soil and the "Red Land" of the surrounding desert.

Ancient Egyptians built monumental architecture that still stands today. The Great Pyramid of Giza, built for Pharaoh Khufu around 2560 BCE, is one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World and the only one still largely intact. The Sphinx, temples of Karnak and Luxor, and the Valley of the Kings demonstrate the engineering prowess and artistic achievement of this civilization.

Egyptian society was hierarchical with the pharaoh at the top as both political ruler and divine intermediary between the gods and people. Below the pharaoh were priests, nobles, scribes, craftsmen, farmers, and slaves. Women in ancient Egypt had more rights than in many other ancient societies and could own property and conduct business.

The Egyptians developed one of the world's earliest writing systems, hieroglyphics, used primarily for religious and monumental inscriptions. The discovery of the Rosetta Stone in 1799 allowed scholars to finally decode hieroglyphics in the early 19th century.

Egyptian religion was polytheistic with hundreds of gods and goddesses. Major deities included Ra the sun god, Osiris god of the afterlife, Isis goddess of magic, and Horus the sky god. The complex beliefs surrounding death and the afterlife led to mummification and elaborate burial practices.
